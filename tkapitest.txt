The publication of scientific research is the cornerstone of the dissemination of knowledge, as well as a fundamental criterion for academic and sci- entific evaluation and career advancement. In many academic commu- nities around the world, the use of reliable scientific metrics is considered important in assessing the quality of individual scientists' contributions. No single metric is appropriate for a comprehensive as- sessment of author-level impact. The choice of specific metrics depends on the purpose and content of the assessment. There is no practical rule about which index to use to measure the diversity of a researcher's pub- lished work, both within itself and among the studies done by the re- searchers. The adapted Simpson Index proposed in this study is the easiest to use compared to other diversity indices (Shannon-Weiner Index, Evenness Index etc.). The values obtained from the adapted Simpson Index are not sensitive to the number of journals, as the num- ber of articles published in the same journal is used instead of the jour- nal number to calculate the numerator or denominator. The use of journal diversity indexes helps a researcher to evaluate the effectiveness of research results by different editors, reviewers and readers.
